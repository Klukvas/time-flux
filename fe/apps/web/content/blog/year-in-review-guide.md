# How to Do a Year-in-Review That Actually Matters

Every December, the internet fills up with year-in-review templates. Most of them ask the same vague questions: "What are you grateful for? What would you do differently?" And most people fill them out once, feel briefly inspired, and forget about it by January 3rd.

Let's do something better.

## Why Most Reviews Fail

They rely on memory. And memory is unreliable. You remember the dramatic moments (the vacation, the breakup, the promotion) but forget the quiet trends that actually shaped your year.

A good review needs **data**, not just feelings.

## A Better Framework

### Step 1: Gather Your Raw Material

Before you reflect, collect. Pull up your:

- **Mood data** (if you've been tracking)
- **Calendar** (scan month by month)
- **Photos** (sorted by date)
- **Chapter markers** (if you use LifeSpan, these are already there)

Don't try to interpret yet. Just lay everything out.

### Step 2: Identify the Chapters

What were the distinct phases of your year? Most people have 3 to 6. Name each one. For example:

- January through March: "The Slow Rebuild"
- April through June: "Finally Clicking"
- July through September: "Travel and Reset"
- October through December: "New Foundations"

### Step 3: Score Each Chapter

For each chapter, rate three things on a scale of 1 to 10:

1. **Energy** - How alive did you feel?
2. **Growth** - Did you learn or improve?
3. **Alignment** - Were you spending time on what matters to you?

This gives you a simple heat map of your year.

### Step 4: Find the Turning Points

What moments changed the direction of your year? Usually there are 2 or 3. A conversation, a decision, a realization. Write them down. These are the hinge points that everything else pivoted around.

### Step 5: Extract One Principle

Not ten lessons. Not five takeaways. One principle that you want to carry into next year. Make it specific enough to act on.

Bad: "Be more present."
Good: "Protect Sunday mornings. No screens before 11am."
